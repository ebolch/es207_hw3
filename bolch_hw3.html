Western Lake Erie’s nutrient concentrations are largely associated with Maumee River input, which varies from year to year due to variation in weather conditions. As a result, we expect that TP, as well as chla, concentration distributions vary by year. Use the function qqmath (from package lattice) to draw normal Q-Q plots of TP and chla concentrations by year. Are the annual concentration distributions closer to being normal?

require(lattice)
## Loading required package: lattice
z<-qqmath(~chla | factor(Year), data = erie, distribution = qnorm, main = "Q-Q Plot",  ylab = "[Chla]",
          panel = function(x, ...) {
            panel.qqmath(x, ...)
            panel.qqmathline(x, ...)
            })
plot(z)

Chla concentration data only exists for 1 year (2013), plotted above. The log normal distribution is skewed, because the values depart from the q-q line at each end, meaning there are more values above and below the mean. In a normal distribution the majority of values would be located on or close to the mean.

If so, is the difference between the two TP concentration distributions more likely to be additive or multiplicative?

The difference is more likely to be multiplicative because the points have a higher slope than approximately 1, not just a higher intercept as would be expected for additive.

  1. Describe the difference in the two distributions in non-technical terms.

The distribution of samples from ODNR has several samples with very high TP concentrations and a total of only 260 samples compared to the NOAA data, which contains only one very high TP sample and has a total of 475 samples. Based on this information, one would think that it’s more likely that you have a very high TP concentration if referencing the ODNR data rather than the NOAA data. This change difference in the samples results in plots of different shapes when compared to an expected normal distribution. The upward curve of the NOAA QQplot indicates that there are more samples away from the average than expected, while the S shaped QQplot from the ODNR indicates that there are extreme values not expected in a normal distribution. This difference of more than expected on the edges, not the average of the data can also be seen in the histograms.

  1. Figure 3.16 explores the dependency of the ozone concentration-solar radiation relationship on wind speed and temperature. Use the same conditional plot to examine the dependency of the ozone concentration-temperature relationship on wind speed and solar radiation, and the ozone concentration-wind speed relationship on solar radiation and temperature.
require(lattice)

WindSpeed <- equal.count(airquality$Wind, 3, 0.25)
Temperature <- equal.count(airquality$Temp, 3, 0.25)
SolarR <- equal.count(airquality$Solar.R, 3, 0.25)
print(
  xyplot(sqrt(Ozone) ~ Temperature|WindSpeed*SolarR,
         data=airquality,
         panel=function(x,y,...){
           #            panel.loess(x, y, span=1, degree=1, ...)
           panel.grid()

         ylab=list(label="Sqrt Ozone", cex=0.6),
         xlab=list(label="Temperature", cex=0.6),
         scales=list(x=list(alternating=c(1, 2, 1))),

print(

)

Both Temperature, and Wind speed appear to have monotonic relationships with the sqrt transformed Ozone concentration, though higher temperature corresponds with higher Ozone, while higher wind speed corresponds with lower Ozone.

For the plot of Ozone Conc-Temperature, in the majority of cases that wind speed increases, the strength of the relationship between Ozone Conc and temperature seems to decrease, as indicated by the decreasing slope of the lines, with the exception of the center row (mid Solar R). This suggests that there may be a relationship, but its unclear. As for the effect of Solar R, on the Ozone-Temp relationship, theres not sufficient evidence to suggest a dependency.

For the plot of Ozone Conc-Windspeed the strength of the ozone-wind relationship appears to increase in strength (steeper slopes) as Solar R increases at high temperatures, but almost appears to do the opposite at lower temperatures, and seems to have no effect a mid-range temperatures. Increases in temperature seem to have a stronger effect on the ozone-wind relationship at high Solar R. In either case I don’t think theres sufficient evidence to suggest a dependency as exists in the original 3.16 plot, where Temp and Windspeed have a clear effect on the relationship between Ozone Concentration and Solar radiation.
